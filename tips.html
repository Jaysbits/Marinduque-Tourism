Before You Go

Travel Tips

Everything you need to know to plan a safe, rewarding, and deeply enjoyable Marinduque experience.

01

Best Time to Visit

November to May is the dry season and offers the best weather for beaches, hiking, and outdoor exploration. The island's undisputed peak experience is Holy Week (March or April) for the Moriones Festival — accommodations fill up months in advance, so book early. December is magical for the Bila-Bila Festival in Santa Cruz.

  • Dry season: November – May (ideal for outdoor activities)
  • Holy Week: Book at least 2–3 months ahead
  • Wet season: June – November (lush, fewer crowds, lower prices)
  • December: festive lantern season in Santa Cruz
02

Getting to Marinduque

The most common route is by RORO (Roll-On, Roll-Off) ferry from Lucena Port (Dalahican) to Balanacan Port in Mogpog — approximately 3 to 4 hours across the Sibuyan Sea. FastCraft vessels are also available for a faster but pricier crossing. Light aircraft connect Manila to Boac Airport on select days.

  • RORO ferry from Lucena (Dalahican) → Balanacan, Mogpog (3–4 hrs)
  • FastCraft from Pinamalayan, Oriental Mindoro → Buyabod, Santa Cruz
  • Light aircraft: Manila → Boac Airport (check seasonal schedules)
  • Bus to Lucena from Manila takes 3–4 hours via SLEX
03

Getting Around the Island

Marinduque has no large public transit system, but getting around is part of the adventure. Tricycles and habal-habal (motorcycle taxis) are the main modes of inter-barangay transport. Multicabs serve major routes between municipalities. For independent travelers, renting a motorbike in Boac is popular and affordable.

  • Habal-habal: most flexible, negotiate fare before riding
  • Tricycles: for short town-to-town trips
  • Multicab: budget option for longer municipality routes
  • Motorbike rental: ~₱500–700/day in Boac (bring your license)
  • Pumpboats: for Maniwaya, Tres Reyes, and coastal trips
04

Where to Stay

Accommodation ranges from budget guesthouses and pension houses in Boac to beachfront cottages at Poctoy and Maniwaya. For an authentic experience, choose a homestay — you'll receive home-cooked meals, insider tips, and genuine island warmth that no hotel can replicate.

  • Budget guesthouses: ₱500–1,200/night in Boac
  • Beachfront cottages at Poctoy: ₱800–2,500/night
  • Homestays: ₱600–1,500/night (often includes meals)
  • Island camping (Maniwaya): coordinate with local boatmen
  • Book 2–3 months ahead for Holy Week
05

Safety Reminders

Marinduque is generally very safe, peaceful, and welcoming to visitors. Exercise standard travel precautions and you'll have a trouble-free trip. The island's communities are tight-knit and protective of their guests.

  • Use reef-safe sunscreen to protect the coral reefs
  • Stay on marked trails when hiking Mount Malindig
  • Inform your accommodation of your daily itinerary
  • Never enter caves without a registered local guide
  • Swim only in designated areas at beaches
  • Keep valuables secure in busy festival areas
06

Cash & Connectivity

Marinduque is not yet a cashless destination. Come prepared with enough Philippine pesos to cover your entire stay, as ATM options are limited outside of Boac. Mobile data signal is acceptable in town areas but weakens in remote beaches and mountain trails.

  • ATMs available in Boac — withdraw before venturing elsewhere
  • GCash accepted at some establishments and markets
  • Download offline maps (Google Maps or Maps.Me) before exploring
  • Globe and Smart both have signal in most municipalities
  • Remote areas (Malindig, Bathala) may have no signal
07

Respect Local Culture

The Moriones Festival is a profoundly religious event rooted in centuries of faith — approach it with reverence and respect. Always ask permission before photographing people, especially those in costume. A few words of Tagalog or the local Marinduqueno dialect go a long way.

  • Dress modestly when entering churches and during religious events
  • Always ask before photographing locals — most are happy to oblige
  • Don't treat the Moriones performers as photo props
  • Participate in the Putong ceremony if invited — it's a great honor
  • "Salamat" (thank you) and "Magandang umaga" (good morning) go far
08

What to Pack

Pack light and practical for this tropical island. Sun protection is essential, and good footwear matters whether you're hiking or navigating wet cave floors.

  • Reef-safe sunscreen and UV-protective rash guard
  • Sturdy closed-toe shoes for cave and mountain visits
  • Lightweight, quick-dry clothing for beach days
  • Rain jacket (especially June–November)
  • Waterproof bag or dry bag for island hopping
  • Portable power bank (electricity can be intermittent in remotes areas)
  • Cash in small bills (₱20, ₱50, ₱100 denominations)
